Multilingual ASR Benchmark & MOSS Caption-Merge Validation

An empirical study on real Emolia speech: (1) comparing ASR models for transcription, timestamps and non-verbal event tags; (2) validating the proposed sentence-level caption-merge prompt format end-to-end with MOSS-TTS-Local 4.55B.

1 · Overview & method

We draw a bounded, language-balanced sample of real clips from the VoiceNet/emolia-thinking corpus (a VoiceNet-annotated subset of Emolia). Audio is streamed from WebDataset shards (tar members {key}.flac) and each clip carries a reference transcript in its {key}.json.

English: 90German: 90French: 60Chinese: 70Japanese: 45Korean: 55Total Exp-1 clips: 410

Experiment 1 runs each ASR model on the sample and measures word/character error rate, timestamp availability and agreement, non-verbal / sound-event tag coverage, and throughput. Experiment 2 takes ~36 clips, transcribes them with sentence timestamps, scores every sentence with the LAION voice models, generates procedural voice (captions), assembles the proposed merged MOSS prompt, and re-synthesises it with MOSS-TTS-Local 4.55B to test whether the format reproduces the original delivery.

2 · Ground-truth: what is and isn't gold

Reference text — REAL. Every Emolia clip ships a reference transcript (text field). We therefore report true WER (word error rate, space-delimited languages) and true CER (character error rate, CJK) against that transcript. Caveat: the Emolia transcript is the corpus-provided transcript, not a fresh human re-annotation, so it is a strong but not infallible reference.
Timestamps — NO gold. Emolia has no word- or sentence-level boundary annotations. We therefore cannot compute timestamp accuracy. Instead we report (a) timestamp availability per model and (b) cross-model timestamp agreement — the mean/median absolute offset between models on identically-recognised words — plus qualitative examples.

3.4 Timestamp agreement (no gold — cross-model)

nvidia/parakeet-tdt-0.6b-v3 vs fluxions/whisperdrz: on 1122 identically-recognised words across 102 clips, the mean absolute word-start offset is 0.527 s (median 0.1 s). The low median indicates the two models place most word boundaries within ~100 ms of each other; the larger mean is driven by a minority of clips where recognition diverges.

3.5 Cross-model transcription agreement

nvidia/parakeet-tdt-0.6b-v3 vs fluxions/whisperdrz on English (89 clips): word-level disagreement 11.6% — consistent with both models sitting near ~10–14% WER against the reference, i.e. they mostly agree with each other and with the gold text.

4 · Experiment 2 — the caption-merge format, validated end-to-end

The proposed fine-tuning format packs an utterance into two fields:

  • instruction — a global description of the speaker (stable timbre, age, gender, register), aggregated across the clip's sentences.
  • text — the spoken content with a per-sentence procedural voice caption in parentheses and real inter-sentence silences as [pause Xs] markers:
    (caption₁) sentence₁ [pause 0.8s] (caption₂) sentence₂ …

Pipeline per clip: Parakeet sentence timestamps → cut each sentence → score it with VoiceCLAP-commercial (57 VoiceNet dims + genuineness + burst blend) and BUD-E-Whisper → Empathic-Insight-Voice-Plus (40 EmoNet emotions) → procedural caption (top-5 VoiceNet, top-3 EmoNet, always Age/Gender/Register/Tempo, genuineness-gated, synonym variety) → assemble the merged prompt with a ~50% pause-dropout policy → re-synthesise with MOSS-TTS-Local 4.55B (sdpa, bf16) → score the re-synth and compare to the original.

4.1 Did the format transfer on the base model? (No — it needs fine-tuning)

Headline: the current, not-yet-fine-tuned 4.55B checkpoint does not interpret the format — it reads the parenthetical (captions) aloud instead of speaking the sentences. Parakeet transcription of the re-synth takes shows a median word error rate of 2.0 against the intended sentences (2.0 = essentially none of the intended words survive), and 91% of takes contain caption vocabulary (“voice”, “register”, “resonance”, “tempo”…) in the spoken output. See the ‘MOSS actually said’ line on each clip below.
Clips synthesised: 30/36 (6 produced only silence/degenerate audio)Re-synth WER vs intended (median): 2.0Caption leakage: 91% of takes

The acoustic voice-profile match is only weak-to-moderate and should be read with care: VoiceNet MAE 1.088/6, orig↔re-synth dimension correlation r=0.223, EmoNet MAE 0.419. Because the VoiceNet/EmoNet scorers read timbre and affect from any speech in the target voice, a clip that recites the caption in roughly the right voice still earns a modest profile score — so these numbers overstate how well the delivery transferred. Per always-on speaker dimension:

Voice Age MAE 1.142Perceived Gender MAE 2.106Register MAE 1.179Tempo MAE 1.352

What this validates: the merge pipeline is sound — sentences, per-sentence captions, the global instruction and real [pause Xs] gaps all assemble cleanly from real signals — but the model must be fine-tuned on the format before it can treat (captions) as non-spoken delivery cues. That is precisely the purpose of the proposed training format.

5 · What worked, limitations, recommendations

ASR — which model for what

  • Timestamps → nvidia/parakeet-tdt-0.6b-v3. It provides word and sentence timestamps from TDT durations, is fast (RTF 0.0505, 2.224 clips/s, 2.64 GB) and accurate on European languages (EN 11% / DE 9% WER). This is the model Experiment 2 relies on for sentence cutting.
  • Non-verbal tags → fluxions/whisperdrz. It tags breath/hesitation/laughter/sigh on 69.5% of English clips and adds word timestamps + speaker diarization, at the cost of ~14× slower inference (RTF 0.6936) and English-only coverage. Use it when you need event tags on English audio, not as a general transcriber.
  • CJK / broad multilingual + environmental sound events remain uncovered here: the models that would fill those gaps (Qwen3-ASR, the VibeVoice soundscape/event models) are not loadable on this transformers stack.

Caption-merge format — did it transfer?

Not zero-shot. On the current, not-yet-fine-tuned 4.55B checkpoint the format does not reproduce the intended delivery: the model reads the parenthetical (captions) aloud instead of the sentences (median re-synth WER vs intended 2.0, caption-word leakage in 91% of takes), and 6/36 clips produced only silence/degenerate audio. The acoustic voice-profile correlation is weak (VoiceNet MAE 1.088/6, r=0.223) and partly an artefact of the scorer reading timbre from any speech in the target voice. The value of the experiment is the opposite of a pass/fail: it confirms the merge pipeline assembles a clean, well-formed prompt from real signals (sentence timestamps, per-sentence captions, aggregated speaker instruction, real inter-sentence pauses) — and it demonstrates why fine-tuning on this exact format is necessary: the base model has no notion that (captions) are cues rather than script. This is the concrete motivation for the proposed training format.

Limitations

  • WER uses the corpus-provided transcript as reference, not a fresh human transcription.
  • No gold timestamps exist; timestamp quality is assessed only by cross-model agreement.
  • whisperdrz is English-only and slow; only 2 of 5 requested ASR models loaded.
  • Exp-2 voice-profile deltas are scored by the same LAION models used to build the captions, so that loop is partially self-referential; the decisive, model-independent evidence is the Parakeet re-synth transcript (caption leakage), and the audio players are provided for manual listening.
  • Re-synth used the base checkpoint with generation settings tuned for short prompts; a format-fine-tuned checkpoint is required to actually validate delivery transfer.
